# ADR-0005 — Complete epistemic lifecycle

- Status: accepted candidate for the full-Core v0.1 draft
- Date: 2026-07-13
- Issue: #23
- Supersedes: the assumption that all epistemic and lifecycle states belong in one `status` property
- Scope: full-Core information model; no full-Core JSON Schema is introduced by this ADR

## Context

The official ADUC program requires seven user-visible states:

```text
unknown
inferred
reviewed
verified
canonical
contested
deprecated
```

The existing experimental mapping profile stores four values in one property:

```text
inferred
reviewed
canonical
contested
```

That model was sufficient for the first mapping-profile experiments, but it overloads different questions:

- how a semantic assertion was established;
- whether it is disputed;
- whether it remains current;
- whether any semantic assertion exists at all.

Putting all seven values into one enum would destroy information. For example, replacing `canonical` with `contested` would hide that the challenged assertion was originally publisher-authoritative. Replacing `verified` with `deprecated` would hide how the assertion had been established. Representing `unknown` as a mapping assertion would require a targetless or invented target.

The full-Core model must preserve immutable assertions, explicit uncertainty and deterministic consumer behavior.

## Decision

### 1. The seven effective states are not one storage property

ADUC separates four concerns.

| Concern | Representation |
|---|---|
| No semantic assertion is available | coverage record with `resolutionStatus: unknown` |
| How an assertion was established | assertion `authorityStatus`: `inferred`, `reviewed`, `verified`, or `canonical` |
| Whether an assertion is disputed | immutable challenge record; an unresolved challenge produces effective state `contested` |
| Whether an assertion remains current | immutable deprecation record; a retired assertion produces effective state `deprecated` |

A consumer may report one of the seven effective states, but it must retain the underlying records and must not flatten them irreversibly.

### 2. `unknown` is coverage, not a semantic mapping assertion

An unknown local field has no accepted semantic target.

A coverage record identifies:

- the source and source version;
- the local reference;
- `resolutionStatus: unknown`;
- the actor and time that recorded the unresolved state;
- a structured reason when known.

It must not contain:

- `semanticTarget`;
- `mappingRelation`;
- mapping confidence.

A later inferred assertion does not mutate the coverage record. It is a new immutable assertion. Historical coverage may remain available for audit.

### 3. Assertion authority uses four ordered but non-equivalent levels

#### `inferred`

The semantic correspondence was generated by automation or proposed without accountable human verification.

Required:

- confidence in the mapping, from 0 through 1;
- identified confidence method;
- at least one evidence reference;
- identified producer and issuance time.

An inferred assertion is never authoritative merely because its confidence is high.

#### `reviewed`

An identified human or accountable review process examined the proposed correspondence and accepted it as reasonable for a declared scope.

Required:

- reviewer identity;
- review time;
- review evidence or review record;
- declared review scope.

Review means accountable examination. It does not prove the mapping through a declared verification procedure and does not grant publisher authority.

#### `verified`

An identified verifier evaluated the mapping using a declared, reproducible verification method and supporting evidence.

Required:

- verifier identity;
- verification time;
- verification method identifier;
- verification evidence;
- verification scope or criteria.

Verification may be performed by a qualified person, organization or deterministic tool accepted by deployment policy. A purely automatic guess cannot call itself verified without a separate verification procedure and record.

Verification does not make the assertion canonical.

#### `canonical`

The publisher or organization authorized to define the meaning of the identified source version publishes the mapping as its authoritative definition.

Required:

- identified source authority;
- evidence linking the issuer to that source authority;
- exact source-version binding;
- issuance time.

Canonicality is an authority claim, not a probability. Mapping confidence is forbidden.

A canonical mapping may still be challenged, deprecated or superseded. Canonical does not mean universally true; it means authoritative for the stated source and version.

### 4. `reviewed`, `verified` and `canonical` remain distinct

| State | Question answered |
|---|---|
| `reviewed` | Has an accountable reviewer examined and accepted the mapping for a stated scope? |
| `verified` | Has the mapping passed a declared evidence-based verification procedure? |
| `canonical` | Has the source authority defined this as the intended meaning for the stated source version? |

A reviewed assertion must not be presented as verified unless a new immutable assertion and verification record are issued.

A verified assertion must not be presented as canonical unless the source authority issues a new immutable canonical assertion.

### 5. `contested` is an effective conflict state

A challenge is a separate immutable record that targets:

- one assertion; or
- a local reference for one identified source version.

An open challenge requires:

- challenger identity;
- challenge time;
- reason;
- at least one evidence reference;
- target assertion or local reference.

An unresolved open challenge produces effective state `contested` for automatic consumption. The original assertion retains its `authorityStatus`.

Resolution is represented by a new immutable resolution record. Challenge records are not edited in place.

Consumers must not silently select a contested mapping for automatic joining, conversion or action.

### 6. `deprecated` is an effective lifecycle state

A deprecation is a separate immutable record targeting a published assertion.

Required:

- deprecating actor;
- effective time;
- structured reason;
- target assertion.

A replacement assertion is optional. When present, it is referenced explicitly. The replacement does not rewrite the deprecated assertion.

A deprecated assertion remains available for historical replay and audit but is excluded from current automatic selection.

### 7. Assertions and lifecycle records are immutable

The following actions create new records:

- inference accepted by a reviewer;
- review upgraded to verification;
- verification published as canonical;
- challenge opened or resolved;
- assertion deprecated;
- assertion replaced.

Reusing an existing identifier with changed status, target, evidence or actor is invalid.

### 8. Confidence rules

| Effective input | Confidence rule |
|---|---|
| unknown coverage | forbidden |
| inferred assertion | required with identified method |
| reviewed assertion | optional; method required when present |
| verified assertion | optional only when the verification method defines a calibrated score; method required |
| canonical assertion | forbidden |
| challenge record | forbidden as mapping confidence; challenge strength may use a future separate vocabulary |
| deprecation record | forbidden |

Confidence always estimates semantic mapping correctness under its declared method. It does not represent source data accuracy, prediction probability, legal permission, publisher authority or trust in the actor.

### 9. Deterministic consumer selection

For one local reference and source version, a conforming consumer must:

1. validate source-version binding;
2. reject malformed or identifier-reused records;
3. exclude assertions with effective deprecation;
4. detect open challenges and incompatible active assertions;
5. report `contested` and block automatic use when unresolved conflict applies;
6. prefer one recognized canonical assertion when it is unconflicted;
7. otherwise consider verified assertions accepted by local verification policy;
8. otherwise consider reviewed assertions accepted by local review policy;
9. otherwise report inferred assertions as candidates under declared confidence policy;
10. otherwise report `unknown` or unmapped;
11. preserve the selected assertion, evidence, authority level, conflict records and lifecycle records in derived output.

The reference profile does not impose universal trust lists or confidence thresholds. Deployments declare them, but they may not hide the input state.

### 10. Canonical conflicts

Two incompatible active canonical assertions for the same local reference and source version produce effective state `contested`, even if no explicit challenge record exists.

A consumer must not choose between them using confidence because canonical assertions must not contain confidence.

### 11. Compatibility and migration

Existing profiles remain interpretable under their original `conformsTo` version.

Direct migration:

| Existing `status` | Full model |
|---|---|
| `inferred` | `authorityStatus: inferred` |
| `reviewed` | `authorityStatus: reviewed` |
| `canonical` | `authorityStatus: canonical` |

The existing `contested` value cannot be migrated automatically because it does not preserve the underlying authority level. Migration requires:

1. recovering or explicitly declaring the original authority level;
2. issuing the corresponding assertion in the full model;
3. issuing a separate open challenge record;
4. preserving the legacy assertion identifier and provenance in migration evidence.

No implementation may guess the missing authority level.

The future full-Core schema will use a new versioned `conformsTo` identifier. Existing mapping-profile documents are not silently reinterpreted under the new schema.

## Rejected alternatives

### One seven-value `status` enum

Rejected because authority, conflict, absence and lifecycle are orthogonal. It would lose information during challenge and deprecation.

### `unknown` as a targetless mapping assertion

Rejected because a mapping assertion without a target asserts no mapping and encourages fabricated placeholders.

### Treat `verified` as a synonym for `reviewed`

Rejected because reproducible evidence-based verification changes consumer behavior and audit requirements.

### Treat `verified` as a synonym for `canonical`

Rejected because evidence and publisher authority are different claims.

### Mutate assertions in place

Rejected because it breaks auditability, signatures, evidence chains and deterministic historical interpretation.

## Consequences

### Positive

- all seven required effective states are represented without overloading one property;
- original authority survives challenge and deprecation;
- unknown fields remain honestly unmapped;
- consumers can make deterministic and policy-aware decisions;
- migration from existing profiles is explicit;
- future signatures and release proofs can target immutable records.

### Costs

- the full-Core schema will require multiple record types;
- consumers must compute effective state rather than read one string;
- migration of legacy `contested` assertions requires human or authoritative input;
- trust in source authorities and verifiers remains deployment-specific.

## Security and privacy impact

- No personal data is required by the model; actor identifiers may be organizational or pseudonymous where accountability permits.
- Confidence must not be used as a substitute for authorization or source trust.
- Open challenges and deprecations must not be suppressed when presenting a mapping to an automated agent.
- Authority recognition remains local policy and must not be inferred solely from a self-declared `canonical` string.

## Follow-up

The next schema task must define separate reusable objects for:

- unknown coverage records;
- semantic assertions with `authorityStatus`;
- challenge and resolution records;
- deprecation records;
- source-authority and verifier evidence references;
- deterministic effective-state reporting.
